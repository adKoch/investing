# Chuck Akre's "Three-Legged Stool" Framework

Chuck Akre, founder of Akre Capital Management, is a highly successful investor whose philosophy is centered on finding "compounding machines." His framework is built on a simple metaphor: a three-legged stool. If any one of the legs is missing, the investment will not stand.

## Leg 1: The Quality of the Business (Exceptional Business)

The first leg focuses on identifying businesses that are structurally superior and earn high returns on capital.

* **High and Predictable ROIC:** The business must consistently generate a high Return on Invested Capital (ROIC).
* **Durable Competitive Moat:** The high returns must be protected by a strong moat (e.g., brand, switching costs, network effects, or cost advantages).
* **Pricing Power:** The business must be able to raise prices over time without losing unit volume, protecting its margins from inflation.

---

## Leg 2: The Quality of the People (Talented Management)

The second leg evaluates the people running the business. Akre looks for managers who treat public shareholders as partners.

* **Integrity and Passion:** Management must possess high integrity, competence, and a genuine passion for running the operating business rather than financial engineering.
* **Shareholder Alignment:** Executive compensation should align with long-term return on capital, not short-term share price targets or asset growth.
* **Outstanding Capital Allocation:** Management must have a track record of allocating capital logicallyвҖ”prioritizing high-return internal projects first, then sensible share repurchases, and avoiding overpriced acquisitions.

---

## Leg 3: Reinvestment Opportunities (The Compounder Runway)

The third legвҖ”often the most overlookedвҖ”is the company's ability to reinvest its free cash flow at high rates of return. 

* **High Reinvestment Rate:** It is not enough for a company to generate cash; it must have productive opportunities to reinvest that cash back into the business at high returns.
* **Long Growth Runway:** The business must have a large addressable market or opportunities to expand (organically or through bolt-on acquisitions) so it can deploy cash for years or decades.
* **Avoid Capital Return Traps:** If a company earns a high return but must pay out all its earnings as dividends because it has no growth opportunities, it is a "cash cow," not a "compounding machine."
