# Terry Smith's Investing Guidelines (Fundsmith)

Terry Smith is a prominent British fund manager, founder of Fundsmith, and often referred to as the "English Warren Buffett." His strategy is built around extreme simplicity, low turnover, and a strict focus on high-quality companies.

---

## The Three-Step Mantra

Terry Smith summarizes his entire investment strategy in three simple steps:

1. **Buy good companies.**
2. **Don't overpay.**
3. **Do nothing.**

---

## Step 1: Buy Good Companies

Smith has a very specific definition of a "good company":

* **High Return on Capital Employed (ROCE):** The company must generate high returns on capital, ideally funded by its own cash flows rather than leverage. Smith seeks businesses that consistently generate ROCE far above their cost of capital.
* **Intangible Assets and Moats:** He avoids businesses that rely on physical assets (like factories or machinery) to grow. Instead, he looks for businesses whose advantages are built on brand names, patents, trade secrets, software, or customer switching costs.
* **Resilience to Competition:** These businesses should have products or services that are consumed regularly, in small quantities, at low unit costs (e.g., consumer goods, medical devices, software). This makes them resilient during recessions.
* **High Free Cash Flow Conversion:** A good company must turn a high percentage of its operating profits into actual free cash flow (FCF) that can be reinvested or returned to shareholders.

---

## Step 2: Don't Overpay

Even a great business can be a poor investment if bought at too high a price.
* **Free Cash Flow Yield:** Smith uses the Free Cash Flow Yield (\(FCF / \text{Enterprise Value}\)) as his primary valuation metric, rather than the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio. He seeks a yield that is attractive relative to risk-free government bonds.
* **No "Greater Fool" Strategy:** He does not buy companies in the hope that someone else will pay more for them later (speculating on multiple expansion). He only buys businesses at prices that make sense based on their cash-generating power alone.

---

## Step 3: Do Nothing (Low Turnover)

Once a wonderful business is acquired at a reasonable price, the best strategy is to let it compound and minimize transaction costs.
* **Minimize Friction:** High trading activity incurs brokerage commissions, spreads, and taxes, which drag down performance.
* **Let Compounding Work:** Good companies defy the law of mean reversion. If a business remains high-quality, selling it to buy another asset introduces unnecessary execution risk.
* **Extreme Concentration:** Fundsmith typically holds only 20 to 30 stocks, concentrating capital in their highest-conviction ideas.
