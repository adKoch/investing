# Economic Moats: Durable Competitive Advantages

This document outlines the concept of an **economic moat**—the structural barriers that protect a company's long-term profits and market share from competitors.

---

## What is an Economic Moat?

Coined by Warren Buffett and heavily emphasized by Charlie Munger, a moat is a company's ability to maintain a competitive advantage that protects its market share and profitability. Without a moat, high returns on capital will naturally attract competitors, driving profits down to the cost of capital.

```
   ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
   │         Competitors             │
   └───────────────┬─────────────────┘
                   │  (Attacking)
             ╔═════▼═════╗
             ║   Moat    ║  <-- Brand, Switching Costs, Network Effects, Cost Scale
             ╚═════╦═════╝
                   │  (Protecting)
   ┌───────────────▼─────────────────┐
   │      High Return Business       │
   └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

## The Four Primary Types of Moats

### 1. Brand and Intangible Assets
Intangible assets include patents, regulatory licenses, and strong consumer brands.
* **Pricing Power**: A strong brand allows a company to charge premium prices. For example, customers pay more for Coca-Cola or Apple products even if generic alternatives exist.
* **Patents & Regulatory Barriers**: Drug patents protect pharmaceutical companies, while waste disposal licenses create local monopolies.

### 2. Switching Costs
Switching costs are the monetary, time, or psychological expenses a customer incurs when changing from one product or service to a competitor.
* **Enterprise Software**: Databases (e.g., Oracle) or accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks) are deeply integrated into workflows. Replacing them is risky and expensive.
* **Financial Services**: Custody banks or clearing houses are difficult to replace due to complex operational integrations.

### 3. Network Effects
A network effect occurs when a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it.
* **Two-Sided Networks**: Credit card networks like Visa/Mastercard become more valuable to merchants as more cardholders use them, and vice versa.
* **Platforms**: Marketplaces like eBay, Etsy, or App Stores benefit from massive numbers of buyers and sellers that competitors cannot easily match.

### 4. Cost Advantage (Scale and Efficiency)
Cost advantages allow a company to offer products at lower prices than competitors, or achieve higher margins at the same price point.
* **Economies of Scale**: Purchasing power and distribution efficiency (e.g., Walmart, Costco) allow large firms to undercut smaller competitors.
* **Process Advantages**: Proprietary manufacturing methods or location-based advantages (e.g., a mine located next to a shipping port).

## Moat Strength vs. Moat Trend

When evaluating a business's moat, investors must distinguish between current strength and the future direction (trend).
* **Wide Moat**: Extremely difficult for competitors to breach (e.g., ASML in EUV lithography).
* **Narrow Moat**: Offers moderate protection but could face disruption over a 5-10 year horizon.
* **Moat Trend (Expanding vs. Stable vs. Eroding)**: A wide moat that is slowly eroding (e.g., traditional cable TV networks) can be a value trap, whereas a narrow moat that is expanding (e.g., a fast-growing software ecosystem) is highly valuable.
