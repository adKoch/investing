# Charlie Munger's Quality Investing Criteria

This document details Charlie Munger's criteria for evaluating the quality of a business, as popularized in *Poor Charlie's Almanack* and Berkshire Hathaway annual letters.

## The Quality Principle

Charlie Munger shifted Warren Buffett's focus from traditional "cigar-butt" value investing (buying mediocre businesses at extremely cheap prices) to **quality investing** (buying great businesses at fair prices).

> *"A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price."*

---

## 1. High Return on Capital Employed (ROCE / ROIC)

The ultimate mathematical driver of long-term investment performance is the business's return on its capital.

* **Reinvestment Rate**: A business that can repeatedly reinvest its profits at high rates of return (e.g., 15-20%) will compound value exponentially.
* **Munger's Rule**:
  > *"Over the long term, it's hard for a stock to yield a much better return than the business which underlies it earns. If the business earns six percent on capital over forty years and you hold it for that forty years, you're not going to make much different than a six percent return—even if you buy it at a huge discount. Conversely, if a business earns eighteen percent on capital over thirty or forty years, even if you pay an expensive looking price, you'll end up with one hell of a result."*

---

## 2. Durable Competitive Advantage (The "Moat")

A business must have protection against competitor encroachment to maintain its high returns.
* **Pricing Power**: The ability to raise prices without losing customers to competitors (e.g., brand loyalty or high switching costs).
* **Intellectual Property & Network Effects**: Patents, proprietary technology, or networks that grow stronger as more users join.
* **Low Cost Production**: The ability to produce or distribute products at a lower cost than anyone else (e.g., economies of scale).

---

## 3. Trustworthy and Capable Management

Munger places heavy emphasis on the alignment of incentives and the integrity of management.
* **Incentive Alignment**: Check how executives are compensated. Do they get paid for growing assets (which dilutes returns) or for improving return on capital?
* **Capital Allocation**: How does management spend the free cash flow? Repurchasing undervalued shares, paying dividends, or paying for overpriced acquisitions?
* **Operational Simplicity**: Munger prefers management teams that keep operations simple and transparent, avoiding complex financial engineering.

---

## 4. Understanding the Circle of Competence

Munger advises to stick to businesses that you can actually understand and analyze.
* **Avoid Complexity**: If a business model is too complex to understand (e.g., complex derivatives, rapidly changing cutting-edge biotech), place it in the "Too Hard" pile.
* **Mental Models**: Apply concepts from physics (critical mass, leverage), biology (ecosystems), and psychology (incentive bias) to evaluate whether the business's advantages are durable.
